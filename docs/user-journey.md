# User journey

<!-- Purpose: describes the intended user flow and the questions to validate in research. -->

## Primary journey: choose one next step

| Stage | User action | Product response | Feeling to support | Research question |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Arrive | Opens the case-study prototype. | Explains that it is a fictional, static concept. | Safe, oriented. | Is the disclosure clear without being distracting? |
| Choose | Selects a broad settling-in goal. | Shows a short, deterministic suggestion. | Less overwhelmed. | Do the goal labels match users' language? |
| Understand | Reads the rationale and checklist. | Explains the suggestion in plain language. | In control. | Does the rationale earn appropriate trust? |
| Act | Marks a first checklist item. | Shows lightweight progress in the page. | Capable. | Is the proposed action realistically doable? |
| Escalate | Has a high-stakes question. | Directs the user to official or professional help. | Protected, not abandoned. | Are limits noticeable at the right time? |

## Edge journeys

- **No matching goal:** show a neutral “start with one small task” message rather than inventing a tailored recommendation.
- **Sensitive request:** do not simulate advice; state the boundary and point to qualified, official support.
- **Low confidence:** explain what is unknown and invite the user to verify current local information.

## Journey assumptions — TODO: validate

1. Users prefer choosing a goal over composing a long prompt.
2. One recommendation plus a checklist feels more actionable than a long resource list.
3. Users can distinguish a planning aid from authoritative guidance when the limitation is explicit.
